Web Component: Force Directed Graph Visualization

Vision

Imagine, our word for thinking about something that isn’t, literally has a reference to the sense I think I employ when I do so: An image is seen. We can picture things in our minds.

My goal in implementing the multilevel layer in this force directed graph web component is to allow people an intuitive understanding of our computers, and (thus) our environments. Imagine your network card as a node on a graph that is the powered on circuitry of your machine.

Now imagine a packet, a unit of data transmission characterized by its ability to make it or not make it. Things being sent from your modem, packets. What does that have to do with a graph? The information arriving on your network card can be drawn as an incoming edge between the cloud and your computer, on a dynamic graph, the information packets can flow past colorful vertices, and take on their colors or images, or a generated picture with icons for each of the functions involved, a highlight of credits, a value tagged with a sort of stack trace of every function that touched it.

Imagine packets materializing out of thin air, pulled to the network card by invisible edges to induce motion. From there we can see where the data goes. Luckily, in Linux, everything is a file, even processes. Even process i/o. Just looking at what goes into and out of a file, we can probably put entire computer science departments out of business.

So now your packet travels from the network card to a process listening on a specific port. This port can have a visual representation of data accumulated and data produced, subject to revision by someone better read on the subject.

Once that’s done, I want to look at source code using data flow graphs. This might assist novice analysts in understanding the behavior of source code, simply by plotting the path data takes through a program’s control statements, repetitions, functions and class hierarchies, all which are themselves graphs, operating on abstract syntax trees, file systems (graphs, thanks to shortcuts), and their operating environments, which in my professional opiniation is a physical graph with our mental and made real graphs overlayed.

Rene Descarte’s candle lurks here. And Thoreau sulking by the pool lurks here.
